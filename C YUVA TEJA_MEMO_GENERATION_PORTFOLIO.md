# Deliverable 2 – Memo Template & Style Guide

To maintain consistency across AI-generated memoranda, I prepared a standardized memo template and style guide that defined the expected document structure, writing standards, and formatting conventions. The objective was to ensure that every generated memorandum communicated underwriting decisions in a professional, explainable, and business-friendly manner.

### Standard Memorandum Structure

Each memorandum follows a standardized structure consisting of:

* Application ID
* Applicant Name
* Date Generated
* Executive Summary
* Positive Factors
* Risk Factors
* Final Recommendation (Approve / Reject)
* Confidence Score
* Next Steps for the Analyst

### Style Guidelines

**Tone**

* Professional
* Neutral
* Analytical
* Free from subjective or biased language

**Length**

* Approximately **200–300 words**
* Concise while preserving all essential decision-support information

**Vocabulary**

* Grade 8–10 readability
* Clear and business-oriented language
* Minimal technical jargon

To improve consistency and support future validation, representative memorandum formats were documented for three common underwriting scenarios:

* Low-Risk (Approve)
* High-Risk (Reject)
* Borderline / Manual Review

These reference templates provide a consistent benchmark for prompt refinement and future quality evaluation.

# Challenges & Learnings

Developing a production-ready memorandum generation workflow presented several engineering challenges. One of the most significant was maintaining consistent output quality from a probabilistic Large Language Model. Financial underwriting memoranda require standardized formatting, objective recommendations, and factual consistency, making output variability unacceptable for production use.

To address this challenge, I adopted an iterative prompt engineering methodology. Prompt templates were continuously refined using structured evaluation criteria and repeated validation across diverse applicant scenarios. Explicit formatting instructions and standardized response structures significantly improved consistency while preserving readability and recommendation quality.

Another challenge involved balancing output quality with operational performance. Increasing prompt complexity often improved explanation quality but also increased response latency. Through performance benchmarking and prompt optimization, I achieved a balance between detailed recommendations and SLA-compliant response times.

This experience reinforced that production AI systems depend not only on model capability but also on disciplined engineering practices such as validation, documentation, monitoring, and continuous improvement.
